## What Constitutes DUI in Nevada?

According to [Nevada law](https://www.leg.state.nv.us/NRS/NRS-484C.html#NRS484CSec020), “driving under the influence” means being impaired to the degree that renders a person incapable of safely driving or exercising actual physical control of a vehicle. The substances covered in Nevada’s DUI laws include alcohol, marijuana, illegal drugs, over-the-counter drugs, and prescription drugs.
In the U.S., drunk drivers with a blood alcohol content of 0.08 or higher are responsible for about 30% of all traffic crash fatalities, according to the [National Highway Traffic Safety Administration](https://www.nhtsa.gov/risky-driving/drunk-driving). Drivers can also be seriously impaired by drugs or a combination of drugs and alcohol.

## What to Do Immediately After a DUI Accident

If you are physically capable and your phone is available, call 911. Take photos of the vehicles involved and the license plates of the cars. Record statements from witnesses and get their contact information. Ask the other driver for his or her contact information and insurance data.
It is not your job to determine whether the other driver is sober; the police will do that. However, share any details you notice about the driver or car – such as slurred speech or drug paraphernalia in the front seat – with your [DUI accident attorney](/about).
If you first require medical attention or hospitalization, jot down your memory of the accident after receiving care. Your account will be helpful to your [DUI accident lawyer](/practice-areas/dui-accidents) as he or she works to build your claim.

## Seeking Damages After a DUI Accident

You may walk away from a DUI accident with no visible injuries, only to discover a few weeks later that you have a neck injury or psychological trauma. Fortunately, Nevada has a two-year statute of limitations to file a personal injury claim. Besides medical fees, you may seek damages for lost wages, pain and suffering, property damage, and punitive damages.
The other driver’s insurance company may try to settle with you. Remember, insurance companies act in their best interests, not yours. ## Were You Partly to Blame?

Nevada’s [comparative negligence law](https://www.leg.state.nv.us/nrs/nrs-041.html#NRS041Sec141) allows the court to reward proportional damages if you are 50% or less responsible for an accident. For instance, let’s say you were driving and texting (**a** **big no-no**) and got hit by a drunk driver. The court may reason that if you hadn’t been texting, you might have been able to avoid the accident, so the judge decides you are 30% responsible. Thus, any damages awarded to you will be lessened by 30%.
If you believe you are partially at fault for the accident, do not admit blame to the other driver or the police. Any statements you make may be harmful to your case. Instead, save your comments for your DUI accident attorney only.

## Wrongful Death Claims

Suppose a loved one died due to a DUI accident. In that case, the family may be eligible to file a wrongful death claim for medical and funeral costs, property damage, grief and sorrow, pain and suffering, loss of companionship, and lost earnings of the deceased.
The deceased person’s spouse and children are typically considered to be the heirs, although parents or siblings can be claimants in certain situations.
