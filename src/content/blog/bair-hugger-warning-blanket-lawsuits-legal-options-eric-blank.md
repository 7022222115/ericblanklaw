## Warming Blankets

A warming blanket is a medical device used to keep a patient warm during surgery or recovery. Warming blankets come in a variety of styles, but most use forced air to circulate warm air around the patient's body.
The primary use of a warming blanket is to prevent hypothermia, which is a condition that can occur when a person's body temperature drops below normal. Hypothermia can lead to a range of complications, including impaired immune function, blood loss, and longer recovery times.

## Bair Hugger

Arizant Healthcare (now 3M) developed and introduced the Bair Hugger warming blanket in 1987 as a medical device to prevent hypothermia in surgical patients. Because of its effectiveness in regulating body temperature during surgeries, the device quickly gained popularity in the medical community.
The Bair Hugger warming system operates by using a blower to circulate warm air through a flexible hose and into a disposable blanket that covers the patient. The blanket is designed to be discarded after each use, reducing the risk of infection and ensuring that the patient is protected from cross-contamination.
The Bair Hugger is available in a variety of sizes and styles to accommodate different patients and surgical procedures. It is particularly useful for procedures where the patient's body temperature is likely to drop, such as joint replacement surgeries, where maintaining a stable body temperature can improve patient outcomes.
The device is widely used in hospitals and surgical centers worldwide, and it has been credited with lowering the risk of complications and improving patient comfort during surgery.

## Health Risks and Potential Complications

The Bair Hugger warming blanket is intended to prevent hypothermia and maintain a stable body temperature during surgeries, but there have been concerns raised about the device's safety and the possibility of serious complications in some patients. Several studies on the potential negative effects and complications of the Bair Hugger warming blanket have been conducted.
One of the most well-known studies, published in the Bone & Joint Journal in 2013, discovered that the Bair Hugger may increase the risk of deep joint infection during hip and knee replacement surgery. Other research has found that the Bair Hugger can increase the risk of surgical site infections, particularly in patients undergoing orthopedic surgery, and that some patients have required additional surgeries to treat infections caused by the device.
While not all patients who use the Bair Hugger warming system will develop complications, the device is known to cause deep joint infections (DJI), burns and skin irritation, infections, allergic reactions, and hematomas. The design of the Bair Hugger has the potential to spread bacteria, increasing the risk of DJIs in some patients, and the disposable blankets used with the Bair Hugger may not be completely sterile, leading to infections. Furthermore, the warming system can cause hematomas, which are blood pockets that form beneath the skin, and patients may develop allergic reactions to the materials used in the disposable blankets or the cleaning products used to sterilize the warming system.
The Bair Hugger warming blanket comes in a variety of models, but the ones at the center of the controversy are the 3M Bair Hugger 500 and Bair Hugger 700 series devices. These models have been the subject of numerous lawsuits and defect allegations, and they are currently at the center of the Bair Hugger multidistrict litigation (MDL). The 3M Bair Hugger 500 and 700 series devices are widely used in hospitals and medical facilities all over the world, especially during orthopedic surgeries.
There have been no official recalls of the Bair Hugger warming blanket to date. Several lawsuits, however, have claimed that the device is defective and should be recalled, while 3M has vehemently defended the device, claiming that it is safe and effective when used as directed.

## Timeline for Bair Hugger MDL

* In 1987, the FDA approved the Bair Hugger as a surgical warming device.
* The first Bair Hugger lawsuit was filed by patient Tommy Walton in 2013, in which he asked for $1 million in damages.
* From 2013 to 2015, over a dozen other patients filed lawsuits in federal courts.
* In December 2015, a federal panel combined the first 14 Bair Hugger lawsuits into a multidistrict litigation (MDL).
* In August 2017, the MDL judge selected five cases for bellwether trials.
* In May 2018, the manufacturer of the Bair Hugger, 3M, won the first bellwether trial in Minneapolis.
* By April 2019, a total of 5,659 Bair Hugger lawsuits had been filed, with 5,036 remaining pending in the MDL.
* In August 2019, U.S. District Judge Joan Ericksen dismissed all remaining cases in the MDL.
* In December 2022, the Bair Hugger class action MDL was formally reopened. Plaintiffs sent a letter of their own to MDL Judge

Ericksen objecting to 3M's proposed schedule for the MDL moving forward and highlighting new scientific evidence.
